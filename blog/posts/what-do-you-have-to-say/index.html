What Do You Have To Say?

By Ben Musser • 2025-09-16

In November 2024, I wrote the first draft of a song called AlgoRhythm. At the time it was nothing more than a sketch — a line in a notebook, a half-muttered melody. Less than a year later, that spark turned into 26 finished songs.

What changed wasn’t me. It was the tools.

From bottlenecks to release valves

Every generation of music has been shaped not just by talent but by the tools available.

The printing press spread hymns and sheet music, creating shared repertoires across continents.

The phonograph pulled voices out of rooms and made them portable.

Magnetic tape let artists edit, overdub, and experiment in ways that live performance never could.

Four-tracks and home studios in the ’80s and ’90s made it possible for kids in garages to sound like full bands.

Digital audio workstations turned laptops into recording studios, changing the scale of what one person could do.

Streaming collapsed distribution — from warehouses and record shops to playlists on your phone.

Each leap removed friction. Each made the bottleneck a little wider. But even in the age of laptops and DAWs, creating a body of work still required time, training, money, and connections.

AI is different. It doesn’t just widen the bottleneck — it blows it open. It collapses the distance between idea and output. It takes what used to be weeks or months and makes them minutes. The things that used to be walls — recording, arranging, distributing — are now buttons.

What AI did for me

This past year, that shift carried me.

AI didn’t “write songs for me.” It caught the fragments that would’ve otherwise slipped away: a Kindle scribble, a voice memo, a late-night journal line. Instead of waiting for the perfect window, I could move ideas forward instantly.

AI tools let me sketch demos that sounded like real songs — enough to hear their bones, to decide if they were worth finishing.

Automations routed my scraps of text into workflows so nothing was lost. My notebooks and voice notes became raw material, not dead ends.

Distribution platforms like DistroKid made the path to Spotify a simple upload, not a maze of industry doors.

Streaming itself meant the moment something was finished, it could exist everywhere at once.

The bottleneck wasn’t can I make this real? anymore. It was is this worth making real?

And that is a profound shift.

The case for struggle

But here’s the counter-take: maybe the struggle was part of the point.

For centuries, art had weight because it cost something. Studio time wasn’t just an expense — it was a ritual. Pressing vinyl or saving up for instruments forced you to choose what mattered most. Scarcity carved meaning.

Without that resistance, you get abundance. And abundance risks dilution. When anyone can release 26 songs in a year, how do you know which ones deserve to last? There’s a haunting thought that friction wasn’t just an obstacle — it was the gravity that gave art its pull.

Not all art is equal

That tension becomes real every time I listen around.

I hear tracks on national radio, backed by big labels, that feel flat. I hear obscure artists with no budget making songs that cut deep. The label doesn’t guarantee quality. The ease of tools doesn’t guarantee mediocrity. Struggle doesn’t guarantee greatness either.

Maybe I’m old. Maybe I’m bitter. More likely, I’m burned. But what I’ve learned is this: everyone deserves a seat at the table — and once they’re seated, the art has to speak for itself.

The equalization isn’t about making all art equal. It’s about giving every voice a chance to be heard, and letting the resonance — not the gatekeepers — decide what matters.

The headspace of finishing

By the time I reached song 26, I felt something unusual: not exhaustion, but closure. A sense that a chapter was finished.

In another era, that body of work would have taken me years, maybe decades. Studio costs, schedules, missed opportunities — any one of those bottlenecks might have killed it. Instead, the songs exist. They’re not perfect. But they’re real. They’re alive.

That’s the release valve. The pressure doesn’t build until you break. It vents, and in the venting, you can move forward.

What excites me

My 26 songs are just one example. But the bigger story isn’t me. It’s the fact that thousands of voices — voices once silenced by access, cost, or time — are finding their way out too.

The printing press democratized reading. The camera democratized memory. The internet democratized distribution. AI is democratizing creation itself.

And here’s the thing: that doesn’t make musicians less important. It makes them more powerful. When the bottlenecks fall away, what rises is intent. Musicians don’t have to spend their energy fighting just to be heard; they can spend it asking deeper questions about what they want to say, and how they want to say it.

Yes, there will always be opportunists chasing a quick buck — that’s always been true. But the creative spirit has never thrived because of scarcity; it has thrived because of curiosity, persistence, and truth. The difference now is that musicians don’t need permission. The tools are in their hands. And with them, the power to push further, to experiment louder, to say more.

It doesn’t mean everything will be good. It doesn’t mean everything will last. But it means the bottleneck is gone, and the flood is here.

The question is no longer can you create? The question is what do you have to say?
